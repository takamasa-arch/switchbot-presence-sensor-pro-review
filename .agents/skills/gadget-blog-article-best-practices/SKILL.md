# Gadget Blog Article Best Practices

## Overview

Use this skill to write gadget blog articles that feel calm, practical, and experience-led rather than promotional. Keep the main workflow in this file, then read [references/style-guide.md](references/style-guide.md) when you need detailed tone rules, section prompts, and rewrite patterns.

## Core Workflow

1. Identify the article type before writing.
   Choose one primary job: hands-on review, comparison, problem-solving guide, or buying judgment. Do not mix all four equally; one article should have one clear decision axis.

2. Start from the lived problem, not the spec sheet.
   Open with the concrete friction the reader already knows, then position the product as a candidate answer. Treat specs as supporting evidence, not the story itself.

3. Reveal the conclusion early.
   Within the first screenful, state who benefits, where the product worked, and what limits remain. Readers of gadget blogs decide quickly whether the rest is worth reading.

4. Convert features into daily consequences.
   For every feature, answer "What inconvenience does this remove?" or "What tradeoff does this introduce in real use?" If that answer is weak, cut or compress the feature mention.

5. Separate evidence types explicitly.
   Mark whether a claim comes from first-hand use, official specs, or inference. When the article blends them, write the boundary clearly so the reader knows what was observed and what was estimated.

6. End with a purchase judgment, not a vague summary.
   Close with "who should buy" and "who can skip" so the article lands on a decision. A gadget post without that closing filter feels unfinished.

## Recommended Article Shape

- Title:
  Lead with the reader's problem or decision, then the product name, then the tested outcome.
- Introduction:
  State the lived frustration, why the product caught attention, and what question the article will answer.
- Early conclusion:
  Summarize the strongest fit, biggest caveat, and overall satisfaction in 3 to 5 short paragraphs or bullets.
- Why this product mattered:
  Explain the exact use case that made the product worth testing.
- What the feature means in practice:
  Translate the core technology or spec into plain-language value.
- Usage environment:
  State room, workflow, connected devices, and what was or was not measured.
- Main experience:
  Cover entry behavior, sustained use, pain points, and the moment the product felt useful or disappointing.
- Concerns and adjustments:
  Record setup sensitivity, false positives, battery anxiety, fit-and-finish issues, or ecosystem constraints.
- Comparison and buying judgment:
  Add a concise table or bullets if comparison helps, then close with buy / skip guidance.
- Summary:
  Restate the practical change in everyday life and the remaining uncertainty.

## Tone Rules

- Prefer calm confidence over excitement. Avoid exaggerated praise.
- Prefer short paragraphs with one idea each. Let the article breathe.
- Prefer "生活がどう変わるか" over "スペックが高い".
- Admit uncertainty when measurement is incomplete. Precision theater weakens trust.
- Use first-person observations to anchor authenticity, but keep the reader's decision as the center of gravity.
- Avoid affiliate-style urgency, empty superlatives, and spec-dump intros.

## Rewrite Checklist

- Cut any paragraph that only restates official specs without lived meaning.
- Move the purchase judgment earlier if the first 20 percent feels too slow.
- Replace generic praise with a scene, comparison, or concrete inconvenience removed.
- Add one explicit caveat for every strong recommendation.
- Check that each heading advances the buying decision.
